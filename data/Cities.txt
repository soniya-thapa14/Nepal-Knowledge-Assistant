CITIES OF NEPAL

KATHMANDU - THE CAPITAL CITY
Kathmandu is the capital and largest city of Nepal, with a metropolitan population of approximately 3 million people. Located in the Kathmandu Valley at an elevation of 1,400 meters (4,600 feet), it serves as the political, economic, and cultural heart of the nation. The city was founded in the 12th century and has been continuously inhabited since ancient times.

Kathmandu is renowned for its rich history and concentration of UNESCO World Heritage Sites. Kathmandu Durbar Square, a complex of palaces, courtyards, and temples built between the 12th and 18th centuries, showcases Newari architecture. The square was the royal palace of the Malla and Shah kings who ruled the city.

Major landmarks include Swayambhunath (Monkey Temple), a 2,500-year-old Buddhist stupa perched atop a hill overlooking the valley; Pashupatinath Temple, one of the most sacred Hindu temples dedicated to Lord Shiva; and Boudhanath Stupa, one of the largest Buddhist stupas in the world and a center of Tibetan Buddhism.

The city is also the commercial hub, hosting the headquarters of most major companies, banks, and government offices. Tribhuvan International Airport, Nepal's only international airport, is located here. Thamel, the tourist district, is famous for its shops, restaurants, and nightlife. Kathmandu faces challenges including air pollution, traffic congestion, inadequate infrastructure, and pressure from rapid urbanization.

POKHARA - THE LAKE CITY
Pokhara is Nepal's second-largest metropolitan city and the capital of Gandaki Province, with a population of approximately 500,000. Located about 200 kilometers west of Kathmandu at an elevation of 822 meters, it is known for its stunning natural beauty and serves as the gateway to the Annapurna region.

The city is situated on the shores of Phewa Lake, the second-largest lake in Nepal, offering spectacular views of the Annapurna range, including Machhapuchhre (Fishtail Mountain), which dominates the skyline. Pokhara is a major tourist destination, attracting trekkers, adventure enthusiasts, and those seeking natural beauty.

Key attractions include Phewa Lake with the Tal Barahi Temple on an island in its center, Begnas Lake, Davis Falls (Patale Chhango), Gupteshwor Cave, the World Peace Pagoda, and the International Mountain Museum. Pokhara is the starting point for treks to the Annapurna Circuit, Annapurna Base Camp, and Poon Hill.

The city is also famous for adventure sports including paragliding, ultra-light aircraft flights, zip-lining, and bungee jumping. Pokhara has a more relaxed atmosphere than Kathmandu and benefits from cleaner air and a more tourist-friendly infrastructure. The Pokhara International Airport, which opened in 2023, enhances connectivity.

LALITPUR (PATAN) - THE CITY OF ARTS
Lalitpur, also known as Patan, is located just across the Bagmati River from Kathmandu and is the third-largest city in Nepal. With a population of around 300,000, it is renowned as the city of fine arts and crafts, particularly metalwork and stone carving.

Patan Durbar Square is a UNESCO World Heritage Site featuring an extraordinary collection of temples, statues, and courtyards. The square contains the ancient Royal Palace with its multiple courtyards and the Krishna Mandir, a stone temple considered a masterpiece of Newari architecture. The Patan Museum, housed in the renovated palace buildings, is considered one of the finest museums in Asia.

The city is predominantly Buddhist and is home to numerous viharas (monasteries) and stupas, including the four Ashoka Stupas at the cardinal points, allegedly built by Emperor Ashoka in the 3rd century BCE. Patan is famous for its traditional artisans who create intricate metalwork, stone sculptures, and wood carvings using techniques passed down through generations.

Other notable sites include the Golden Temple (Hiranya Varna Mahavihar), Mahabouddha Temple (Temple of a Thousand Buddhas), and Kumbheshwar Temple. Lalitpur has maintained its traditional character better than Kathmandu while developing modern amenities and educational institutions.

BHAKTAPUR - THE CITY OF DEVOTEES
Bhaktapur, meaning "City of Devotees," is an ancient Newari city located about 13 kilometers east of Kathmandu. With a population of approximately 80,000, it is the smallest of the three major cities in the Kathmandu Valley but is exceptionally well-preserved and feels like a living museum.

Bhaktapur Durbar Square is a UNESCO World Heritage Site containing the 55-Window Palace, built in 1427, and the Golden Gate, considered the most beautiful and richly carved specimen of its kind. The city is famous for its pottery, with Pottery Square being a major attraction where traditional methods are still practiced.

Notable landmarks include Nyatapola Temple, a five-story pagoda that is the tallest temple in Nepal; Bhairavnath Temple; and Dattatreya Square. The city is also known for its juju dhau (king curd), a sweet yogurt, and traditional Newari architecture with brick-paved streets and carved wooden windows.

Bhaktapur has strict regulations on vehicles in the old city area, making it more pedestrian-friendly and preserving its medieval atmosphere. The city relies heavily on tourism and traditional crafts, including wood carving, pottery, weaving, and thangka painting.

BIRATNAGAR - THE INDUSTRIAL CITY
Biratnagar, located in the southeastern Terai region, is Nepal's second-largest city by area and serves as the industrial hub of the country. With a population of approximately 240,000, it is the headquarters of Koshi Province and is located just 6 kilometers from the Indian border.

The city is the center of Nepal's industrial activities, hosting jute mills, sugar mills, rice mills, and various manufacturing plants. Biratnagar is well-connected by road to India and has an airport with domestic flights. The city plays a crucial role in Nepal's trade with India and is a major commercial center.

Notable places include the Kali Mandir, Baraha Kshetra (a Hindu pilgrimage site), and various industrial establishments. The city has grown rapidly due to its economic importance and proximity to India. Biratnagar Airport serves as an important domestic hub connecting the eastern region to Kathmandu.

BIRGUNJ - THE GATEWAY TO NEPAL
Birgunj is a border city in the Terai region adjacent to Raxaul, India. With a population of around 200,000, it is the commercial capital of Nepal and handles the majority of Nepal's trade with India. Located in Madhesh Province, it serves as a crucial entry point for goods entering Nepal.

The Birgunj Customs Office is one of the busiest in Nepal, processing imports and exports. The city is connected by the Tribhuvan Highway to Kathmandu and has an airport. Major industries include pharmaceuticals, sugar, and manufacturing. Birgunj is also known for the Gadhimai Temple, where a controversial animal sacrifice festival occurs every five years.

JANAKPUR - THE SACRED CITY
Janakpur, located in the Terai region, is the capital of Madhesh Province and has a population of approximately 170,000. The city holds immense religious significance as the birthplace of Goddess Sita and the location where Lord Rama married her, making it a major pilgrimage site for Hindus.

During Ram Navami and Vivah Panchami, the city hosts large religious gatherings with thousands of pilgrims from Nepal and India.

Janakpur is known for Mithila art, a traditional style of painting practiced by women of the region. The city also features in the ancient Hindu epic Ramayana and has deep cultural connections with the Mithila region of India.

HETAUDA - THE PLANNED CITY
Hetauda, the capital of Bagmati Province, is located in the Makwanpur district with a population of about 150,000. It is one of Nepal's few planned cities and serves as an industrial and commercial center. The city is accessible via the Tribhuvan Highway and has a more organized layout than most Nepali cities.

Hetauda Industrial District houses cement factories, chemical plants, and other industries. The city is also a gateway to Chitwan National Park and benefits from its strategic location between the mountains and Terai. Notable places include Martyr's Memorial Park and various nearby natural attractions.

DHARAN - THE EDUCATION HUB
Dharan, located in the foothills of the Mahabharat Range, is a major city in eastern Nepal with a population of around 150,000. Known for its educational institutions, particularly the B.P. Koirala Institute of Health Sciences, it serves as a regional center for education and healthcare.

The city has a pleasant climate and serves as a gateway to hill destinations in the east. Dharan is known for its cleanliness and organized layout. Key attractions include the Pindeshwor Temple, Dantakali Temple, and Bhedetar hill station nearby. The city has a significant Gurkha military connection and is home to many retired and serving Gurkha soldiers.

BUTWAL - THE COMMERCIAL CENTER
Butwal, located in Lumbini Province, is a fast-growing city with a population of approximately 140,000. It serves as a major transportation and commercial hub in western Nepal, located at the junction of highways leading to India, Pokhara, and other regions.

The city is known for its bustling markets, modern infrastructure, and rapid development. Butwal is close to Lumbini, the birthplace of Buddha, and serves as an access point for pilgrims. The city has developed significantly due to its strategic location and serves as a gateway to the western hills.

NEPALGUNJ - THE WESTERN GATEWAY
Nepalgunj, located in Lumbini Province near the Indian border, has a population of about 140,000. It serves as a major transportation hub for western Nepal and the gateway to the remote Karnali region. The city has an important airport with flights to Kathmandu and remote mountain districts.

The population is diverse, with significant Muslim and Hindu communities. The city is known for its trade activities and serves as a supply point for goods heading to remote areas. Bageshwari Temple and Bulbuliya Tal are notable local attractions.

BHARATPUR - THE TOURISM BASE
Bharatpur, the headquarters of Chitwan district and Bagmati Province, has a population of approximately 140,000. The city serves as the gateway to Chitwan National Park, Nepal's first national park and a UNESCO World Heritage Site famous for one-horned rhinoceros and Bengal tigers.

Bharatpur has grown significantly due to tourism and has developed good infrastructure including hotels, restaurants, and transportation facilities. Gautam Buddha International Airport, opened in 2022, is located near the city and serves as an international entry point.
